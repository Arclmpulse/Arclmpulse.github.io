Video Game Reviews

A compilation of all of the games I've played over the years! Reviews contain spoilers. I've roughly ranked them in order using a 20 point scale out of 10. They are also roughly in the order I'd have them ordered from best to worst of each section, but I will admit that there's likely personal bias in ranking them in each section.

I've written this like the way I would speak it - a bit less formal than my actual website, haha. Some cussing and other abbreviations.

Still WiP - I'm thinking of adding a nav for specific series and games, though I'm trying to get all the content in first.

10/10

Chrono Trigger (1995)

SNES/DS/PC

SquareSoft / Square Enix

    My favourite game of all time!

    What Chrono Trigger did that was so revolutionary with a story in a game wasn't some incredible plot twist, or heavy themes that it executed on well, it was its pacing and replayabilty with its multiple endings. At no point in the plot did I ever feel bored, or found myself skipping dialogue for the sake of "going through the motions," I was hooked, I wanted to know what happened next, I didn't want to put this game down. Likable cast of characters with wonderful character arcs, a time travel plot that didn't really have too many plotholes, and a beautiful message that you should always be able to try and change what you can control in your life and to accept that you can't always control everything.

    Talking a bit more about the characters, all of the main ones had great arcs barring Crono, who was the silent protagonist of the crew. Marle and her arc of growing up and maturing to become the next Queen, Lucca's motivation of being an inventor stemming from her trauma of being the reason why her mother is handicapped, Frog/Glenn's regret of being unable to save his best friend/mentor, Robo and his existential crisis as a robot, Ayla uniting the tribes, and even Janus and learning to let go of his past. They were a charming cast of characters, and I appreciated every one of them.

    Lavos is techically the main villain of the game, but they're kinda just the apocalypse. I'd consider Queen Zeal to be the main villain of the game, and I thought they were a fantastic villain. Her descent into insanity from getting drunk off power was a little disturbing, especially after the reveal that Janus/Magus is her son and a large part of the reason why he's also messed up in the head.

    The world and its setting was fantastic, I loved exploring all the different time periods and seeing what was different about them. The fact that items maturing over time periods as a gameplay mechanic was cool too. Combat was a unique take on Square's tried and true FF style, with techs replacing magic and other miscellaneous abilities and it was always interesting to see how your characters could link up. If you've played other JRPGs though, you should take to it like a fish in water.

    The OST of the game is one of my favourite ever, Wind Scene in particular never fails to send a wave of nostalgia through me. Some other iconic hits were Corridors of Time, and Secret of the Forest, if you'd like to check them out.

    I think that's what makes it such a special game too - the fact that it just does everything excellently. Plot, gameplay, music, characters, it has it all. A game that has such an incredible amount of charm and a strange feeling of melancholy whenever I replay it, it's an absolute masterpiece.

Red Dead Redemption 2 (2018)

PC

Rockstar Games

    Somehow, Rockstar outdid themselves.

    The story of this game is pretty legendary. I do think it does go through some slow patches unlike RDR1 where the pacing stayed consistent throughout, (the island, anyone?) but the climax in the plot is one of the most iconic ever in my opinion. The hideouts changing and reflecting the current state of the gang was kinda sad. And of course, I don't think anyone can ever forget the ending to this game, Arthur sacrificing himself to give John and Abigail a chance to live the life Arthur couldn't with Mary really was his redemption. The epilogue setting up the events of RDR1 was fantastic too, brought everything full circle.

    Arthur Morgan is my favourite video game protaganist ever, his arc from starting out as a loyal thug who dreamed of being someone worthy to be with the girl he loved to becoming exactly that without realizing it by the end was incredible. His death was brutal, and we all knew it was coming if you played RDR1 before. What makes Arthur such an interesting character to me is that he genuinely had an artist's soul with his incredible sketches and his appreciation for the beauty of the world around him. Yet it's because of the circumstances of how he grew up in and joining a gang as a child that made him have to live as an outlaw, which was such a waste of talent.

    Micah is nowhere close to being one of my favourite villains, but they were necessary and perfect for the plot. A rat that turned most of the members of the gang on Arthur and pandered to Dutch's overinflated ego, they were the biggest reason why the gang imploded. Just a guy that drove you insane by watching them, fantastic character all around.

    Talking about some more characters, Dutch losing his sanity over the course of the game was really sad to see - I think he's one of the best characters in the game, though it is kinda hard to like him. Another standout character was Sadie - her friendship with Arthur is one of the most well written platonic relationships I've seen in a game as two broken people who could relate to each other better than most in the gang. Seeing Bill and Javier during happier times was also nice to see, though with the plot of the game devolving into what it did, was kinda depressing.

    Gameplay is one of the most impressive I've ever seen. The scale of the world, the amount of random events and sidequests you can do, how alive everything feels, it's just one of the most well made maps I've ever seen in a game. Saint Denis is up there for one of the most impressive cities ever made in a video game. The immersion is incredible with the animations and the little minigames you can do with hunting/fishing among other things, it just feels like you can do anything you want in the game.

    OST was fantastic. American Venom is my personal favourite theme from the game, probably the most iconic theme in the game too. A lot of old-timey country and folk songs that were prevalent in this era too, awesome all-around.

    Arguably the best game I've ever played, I have no idea if Rockstar will ever make a game like this again. It'll be hard to surpass, for sure.

The Witcher 3: Wild Hunt (2015)

PC

CD Project Red

    One of the best games I've ever played.

    All-star lineup of characters in this game, mixing in fan favourite game-canon characters like Roche, Thaler, Letho, etc with the usual book cast. Yen and Ciri finally make their appearance in this series too.

    Writing is up there for one of the best I've seen. The ever present theme of "the lesser evil" from the Witcher series can be found in many quests. Bloody Baron quest line was a masterclass of how to diverge plotlines based off your decisions, and have them converge at a point. Only complaint was that Eredin was underwhelming as the main antagonist, and I do think the plot falls off after the Battle of Kaer Morhen. 4 endings and I like Witcher Ciri and Nilfgaard winning the best, it just made sense.

    Geralt is his usual self, deadpan sarcasm as he gets forced into the trolley problem way too often. They did a great job with him in this one, love his little one-off voice lines and comments. A lot of people call Yen a bitch, but honestly I always thought her sassiness and pettiness was hilarious. I've never really been a fan of Triss as a book fan, but this is probably my favourite iteration of her in the games. Ciri too, was a great add. The father/daughter moments we got from her and Geralt were very wholesome, loved it.

    Combat was fun, though it got repetitive once you figured it out. Still though, one of the most fluid and responsive sword combat systems in a game I've ever seen, it was objectively pretty impressive for its time.

    The locations were fantastic. Novigrad, Velen, Skellige were all fun to explore, especially Skellige. Velen as a war-torn swamp, Novigrad as an unstable region about to explode in war, Ard Skellige having their own share of political turmoil and having some beautiful set pieces on the islands, there wasn't a single region I really disliked. All very distinct.

    That's not even touching DLC. Hearts of Stone told one of the best stories in a game I've seen; I actually preferred the plot of this expansion to the main story. Iris and Olgeird are two of the best characters in the game imo, which is saying something considering all the incredible characters already. Gaunter o'Dimm was a fucking terrifying villain, the soul wager at the end had me stressed all throughout the sequence, I was actually nervous to see the game over screen, thought they would do something unique with it. Nice message of you should always be careful what you wish for.

    Blood and Wine could've been a separate game entirely. Toussaint is probably my favourite world in a video game, ever. I never wanted to leave that expansion, everything about the world was magical to me. Plot was not as good as HoS or the main game, but it was fine. Detlaff and Syanna were nothing special as villains, though I do like the potential ending where the sisters reunite and bury the hatchet. Also, if you romanced Yen in the main game and read the books, I'll say that the epilogue of the DLC is one of the most satisfying conclusions to a character ever.Geralt finally retires with Yen 🥹

    OST was absolutely beautiful. Some of my favourites include Fields of Ard Skellige, Kaer Morhen, You're... Immortal?, Blood and Wine, and especially The Slopes of the Blessure. Latter fills me up with a sense of nostalgia every single time, I'm not sure why.

    Eredin and the Wild Hunt were really the only complaints I had about the game. And it wasn't like they were bad villains, but the victory over them kinda just felt... underwhelming? To elaborate further, they've been hyped up for so long as the harbingers of the apocalypse, yet defeating Eredin felt very hollow and rushed, mostly because Ciri immediately rushed off to deal with the conjuction of the spheres. I actually think Avallac'h being the final boss would've been a brilliant twist and I'm a little sad CDPR chose not to go through with it.

    Very complete game, only some nitpicks I have from the plot. But I feel as though the rest of the game is far good enough to compensate.

Metal Gear Solid 2 - Sons of Liberty (2001)

PS2

Konami

    A game that was far, far ahead of its time.

    Plot is legendary. A game that revolves around the idea of digital/media censorship and how propaganda and selectively limiting the knowledge people are fed can control the masses effectively is something that continues to be true today. Raiden is one of the most deeply complex characters I've seen in a video game, with his history as a child soldier being the root of his trauma as a military soldier. And of course, there was also the fact that his entire life was controlled by the military without his knowledge, including the girl he fell in love with. It was interesting to see how much the game revolved around Raiden's unwillingness to get help for his mental issues in a period of time when mental health was an afterthought, makes you wonder how this game released in 2001.

    An interesting cast of characters: I thought Rose - Raiden's fiancee - was a good character. I did like her story of being someone who was meant to spy on Raiden at first but genuinely fell in love with him. Solid Snake/David not being the main character of the game and being the "mentor" was something that a lot of people hated at the time, but I thought it worked out beautifully. Him being the one to ground Raiden, and help him move forward with his life was nice to see. It was also kinda Kojima's way of telling the fans, "move on from Solid, lol." Shame Konami kept making Kojima milk the shit out of the series...

    Colonel/AI was one of the craziest villains I've ever seen. Raiden slowly piecing together that the person who was guiding him throughout Big Shell was not all it seemed was fantastic, and the moment it all clicks for him that he's demo running a VR sim for future soldiers at Big Shell was perfect. I think what made them such a great villain is how they weaponized Raiden's trauma and quite literally gaslit him and victim blamed him, it was fucked up to watch as poor Raiden started to question everything.

    And then you had Solidus Snake as the secondary antagonist. The fact that they were somehow kind of a victim in this is wild to me, especially since they're the main reason why Raiden is fucked up. Great character, they really paralleled Solid/David's Big Boss for Raiden.

    Gameplay was not that much more different than MGS1, some nice improvements like holdups, moving bodies, and shooting cameras were included, but it is still just as clunky. But really, playing MGS for the combat is a mistake imo, its more like an incredible cinematic story with FPS and stealth elements.

    Some of the most iconic MGS themes come from this game, actually. Yell Dead Cell is a classic, Opening Theme, The World Only Needs One Big Boss! and Tanker Incident are also fantastic. And Can't Say Goodbye to Yesterday is another great song, this one with lyrics. Fitting song to close out the game.

    This was the game I point to that video games can be a form of art, and it's a game that keeps aging like fine wine. A beautiful overarching message that people should ultimately take what they've learned and pass it on to the future generations, MGS2 really was a special game.

Fallout: New Vegas (2010)

PC

Obsidian / Bethesda Game Studios

    Interestingly enough this game did not get good reception when it dropped. The power of Doc Mitchell's spinning head and crashes, I guess...

    New Vegas is a very unique game. It feels like a sequel to the originals Fallout 1 and 2 with the amount of returning characters, factions, and easter eggs and references. Yet it's also a great game as a standalone; you don't need to know all of these things to appreciate it.

    The writing in this game is incredible. From the moment Benny put two bullets in your head in the intro cutscene to the Battle of Hoover Dam, I was hooked from minute one. The amount of branching sidequests with different endings is massive, and what's crazy is how the vast majority of them play into the ending for the faction they represent that you'll get. Your decisions genuinely matter in this game, and some might even come to help you out in the final quest.

    The craziest thing about this game is that there is no main villain after the first act. YOU get to choose who the villain is. You can team up with NCR from Fo2. Or a billionaire who survived the nuclear war and owns the Strip currently. Or a guy named Caesar and his wannabe Roman Empire that happens to control most of Southwest USA. Or you could decide, fuck it, I'm taking over Vegas!

    Honestly, the world itself is the weakest aspect of it. Vegas pales in comparison to both DC and Boston in Fo3 and Fo4, and I honestly didn't have much fun exploring the desert. That being said, it was very interesting to see Obsidian's take on post-post apocalypse like how the NCR aren't really as good as they seem to be on the surface, while Bethesda focuses more on post-apocalypse.

    DLCs are all fantastic. Honest Hearts gives a look into how tribal life can thirve in a reset environment, and gave us one of the best side-stories ever in the Survivalist - I recommend reading the wiki entry on him, there's nothing I can do but salute him. Old World Blues was hilarious with the voice acting, and the twist of Mobius becoming a villain to stop the Think Tank from experimenting on post apocalyptic America was fantastic. Controversial opinion maybe, but Dead Money is probably my most favourite DLC ever, the major characters in this expansion and how they tied the theme of letting go beautifully for all of them (including yourself with the gold bars at the end, haha) was done perfectly. I wasn't huge on Lonesome Road, but it did give the Courier a backstory with Ulysses.

    Gameplay mechanics are much improved. Iron sights, more gun variety, weapon mods, and the faction reputation system and disguises especially were awesome. I think it's criminal Bethesda never brought the disguise system back.

    Inon Zur did a nice job with the score in this one, though it is hard to think of an iconic theme in this game. If anything, the haunting song from Dead Money - Begin Again - is the one that stands out to me.

    It's a shame that Obsidian never got to fully flesh out the Legion, but they delivered an incredibly ambitious game within a short timespan and passed with flying colours.

Silent Hill 2 (2001)

PS2

Konami

    In my opinion, the most iconic horror game of all time!

    The story in this game is one of my favourite ever in a game, and it's a really fascinating one as the game slowly reveals to you what James Sutherland did to his wife years ago, and why he came to Silent Hill. It poses a fascinating question. What should James Sutherland's fate be, for killing his terminally ill wife? And I love how the decisions you make in the game, how you play, how much time you spend with certain characters, all influences the ending of the game.

    James is honestly the main antagonist of the game, since what you see at Silent Hill is a byproduct of his guilt for his murder. And James is a deeply complex and fascinating character since you could definitely argue they are a monster, or that they were a good person who made a terrible mistake. There's definitely leeway to argue for either - it depends on your morality.

    Felt bad for a lot of the characters in the game, honestly. Angela's past was really fucked up, Eddie's as well, but for the opposite reason. Mary didn't deserve what happened to her either. Just a lot of tragic plotlines you see the aftermath of, it's really sad.

    Gameplay really made the most out of the poor shooting mechanics of this era, I'll say that. The puzzles were creative, loved them. The ambience, the unsettling set pieces throughout the game, the realization you have when you begin to see that James is quite mentally ill and not right in the head and it getting reflected in the settings he found himself in in Silent Hill was incredible.

    I personally believe the "In Water" ending is canon. I don't think James was a terrible person judging how he treated most people at Silent Hill, but I do believe he lied to himself constantly and I don't think he was mentally strong enough to survive without Mary.

    OST was beautiful. I have a lot of themes I love from this game, Promise (Reprise), Promise and Theme of Laura are all up there for some of my favourite in the games I've played, just hauntingly melancholic.

    There is no game quite like Silent Hill 2, and the fact that this game still sparks debates and conversations really is a testament to how good it was.

The Last of Us (2013)

PS4/PC

Naughty Dog

    One of the best stories a game has ever told, in my opinion.

    TLOU was an emotional ride from start to finish, Joel's development from a gruff, antisocial "smuggler" to a doting father who gave up humanity's best chance for a cure because he couldn't let Ellie go is one of the most infamous character arcs in all of video games. Ellie's own backstory and development was honestly depressing a bit, as she sees and experiences a lot of fucked up things first hand over the course of the game.

    A lot of characters come and go, and often met tragic ends. Tess had great chemistry with Joel, but sacrificed herself. Henry and Sam's deaths were devestating - it was obvious what was gonna happen as soon as Sam asked Ellie those questions, yet I still wasn't ready to see that scene. Liked Tommy just fine in this game. And if you've played Left Behind, then you'll know about Riley, and man, just so sad. Poor Ellie :(

    TLOU tends to have villains for each mini arc, though I suppose you could definitely argue that Marlene is the main antagonist of the game. They weren't given much time to shine as a character, but you could definitely tell they were conflicted by having to kill Ellie to get the tumour out of her brain and every scene they were in felt impactful, which is a rarity in most games. Overall a good character.

    Gameplay was fantastic. Melee is a viable option with Joel being built like a Navy SEAL, shotgun and the rifle are probably what you'll default to. Very similar to Uncharted's gameplay, unsurprisingly. I believe this game built off the first 3 Uncharted games, and you could tell that stealth really got a revamp for this game - it's much more viable in this one, and is often recommended if you can help it. The crafting and upgrades system was fantastic as well, just great all around.

    Last of Us had some great themes, Gustavo Santaolalla did a great job of making it as bleak and dreary as possible. I think the main theme and Vanishing Grace are standouts of the score.

    A game that focused a lot on the darker elements of humanity like cannibalism, slavers, smuggling and more, it's honestly not the zombies that are the monsters in this game, it's the people who are still normal. Focusing on what society would likely devolve into with an emotional plot, Last of Us truly was a masterpiece, and I do believe it is Naughty Dog's magnum opus.

The Legend of Zelda: Majora's Mask (2000)

N64/GCN

Nintendo

    Sequel to a beloved game!

    The story in Majora's Mask is incredible. When you first reach the alternate universe of Termina and are suddenly presented with a 3 day in-game time limit, you'll probably feel like you're gonna hate this game. But you keep playing, you keep grinding away. You learn about so many NPCs that live in Clock Town that you end up deciding to help them. And you go to each of those 4 major areas from Termina Field, helping to fix the land and area there as you make their lives better and take out the major threat in each region.

    And then you have to reset the cycle, as you go back to the start of the 3 day cycle. It broke my heart, that they'll never remember all the things you've done for them in a different time and learned about - especially in those four major zones.

    Skull Kid was a sad villain honestly, guy just wanted to make friends and ended up causing the apocalypse by accident when they fell into this parallel universe. Very childish and acts kinda cartoonishly evil, but they also remind me a lot of a kid that was never taught how to be a moral, upstanding citizen of the Lost Woods, and that in itself made me feel sorry for the guy.

    Gameplay was fantastic, the swimming mechanics were chef's kiss. The mask system was wonderful and gave you different ways to fight should you choose to. What few items there were, were all iconic. Only 4 dungeons and bosses, and I think that's what made all of them so memorable, Snowhead is one of my favourite in the series, and I don't think I'll ever forget the twist of the Stone Tower Temple.

    Majora's Mask had some wacky themes that I loved. Stone Tower Temple, the inverted version, and the Boss Theme were the ones I remember best.

    A sneakily dark game that revolved a lot around death, nihilism, and a grim reminder that it's impossible to save everyone, it's also a game about hope and forging everlasting bonds that can transcend time. A game that I love dearly, very few games match the amount of character and love that was clearly poured into it. I think it's the best in the series and one of the best of all time.

Final Fantasy 6 (1994)

SNES/GBA

SquareSoft / Square Enix

    Maybe a slightly hot take because of the latter part I'm about to say - I think this is the best FF, and it's not close.

    The plot of the game is legendary. How many games can say that the main villain actually accomplished their goal? And the entire second half of the game is non-linear, you could just pull up to the Tower basically whenever you wanted - re-recruiting everyone is completely optional.

    What's interesting about this game is that there is no main protagonist. The closest you'll get is either Terra or Celes, and both are fantastic characters - two of the best Square has ever put out. Terra as she starts as someone who wants to learn what love is, to getting teased with many romantic relationships, to discovering love by adopting the orphans in Mobliz as she became their surrogate mother. And Celes, as she goes from someone with such a low sense of self-worth that she tried suicide shortly after Kefka ruined the world, but found a reason to live again after seeing Locke's bandana on the seagull as she eventually reunites the party to take down Kefka, fueled by the hope she could one day reunite with the friends she betrayed.

    And that's not all. Locke's own story of his own trauma from losing loved ones and learning to forgive himself with his story coming full circle with his romance with Celes in the ending, Edgar's sacrifice to Sabin with the double headed coin, Relm and Shadow's own history as father and daughter, Setzer and finding a passion in life again, the cast of this game was absolutely stacked.

    Kefka is one of the most iconic villains in gaming, honestly. Quite literally the Japanese Joker, the guy is completely unhinged. A clown with a nihilistic god complex, they actually succeed in making the world a hellhole that they rule over. Taking them down was one of the most satisfying moments in gaming for me.

    OST was outstanding. Nobuo Uematsu went all out in this one, Decisive Battle, Terra, Locke and Celes' Theme, and Searching for Friends are beautiful. And imo, his single greatest work ever was in this game - Dancing Mad is legendary, and for good reason. Third movement is my personal favourite part of the 17 minute overture, but the fourth is the most iconic part of the track.

    Gameplay wasn't anything unique or special, it was just classic FF through and through. This game didn't innovate much, but it did tell one of the best stories ever from a JRPG.

Uncharted 4 (2016)

PS4/PC

Naughty Dog

    Oh my, this game got robbed as GOTY back in 2016. Also, the only game in the series without some weird supernatural thing, very nice.

    Best game in the series, and I don't think it's close. Plot was the best of the series, with the lore of the Drake brothers chasing after the last thing their mother was researching when they were kids (Henry Avery's treasure/Gunsway Heist), them reuniting long after Nate decided to retire from "the life," and how Nate risks his marriage to go on one last crazy adventure with his older brother was perfect. Especially since his actions have consequences later down the line, the scene where Elena confronts Nate in Madigascar was so raw, and incredible.

    Rafe Adler is the best villain in the series. His inferiority complex to the Drake Bros and how he was self conscious of just being a nepo baby making him desperate to find this treasure so he could claim he accomplished something in his life without someone else doing it for him, perfection. Unhinged as well, but it felt far more personal than any other villain in the series. The ending to the game was perfect as well, Nate realizing that his treasure is the people around him in his life is exactly why he survived on Avery's burning ship, while Rafe couldn't treasure his own life enough to let go of it.

    Combat and gameplay is the best in series, stealth is 100% viable in this game, and there's a good chance you can take out up to half the enemies in an area without getting detected if you're really good. The gunplay is much improved, the enemies feel perfectly balanced now, perfection. The platforming also took a step up with the climbing spike and just how fluid the movement is, it's so fun to just run around and swing on ropes when you're in those sections.

    OST was great - bringing in Henry Jackman will tend to do that, I will say. I loved the combat themes - Cut to the Chase and New Devon are so ridiculously good, especially the latter. When the low brass kicks in, I swear I start shooting better in those last chapters.

    It's kinda weird to put this as a perfect game, because I do feel like it lacks something outstanding that I think other 100% games often have, but I do think it's game with very few flaws, and that in itself deserves praise.

9.5/10

Xenoblade Chronicles 1 (2010)

Wii

Monolith Soft

    ITs rEYn tImE!!!

    This game often gets raved about by JRPG fans, and it's no surprise why. XC1 has a fantastic plot that stayed strong from start to end with a compelling narrative starting with the invasion of Colony 9, to the game becoming a plot that revolved around the cycle of retaliation in war. Shulk was a great main lead, I know he looks like a twink but he's really a chad and honestly a great character. Fun cast overall though I do feel like a majority of the main cast don't really get much focus in the 2nd half of the game, it was a game that revolved mostly revolved around Shulk and Seven, and that is something I do think the game didn't do that great. I know people say Melia got done dirty but there was always only ever one girl for Shulk, and that's Seven haha. Happy a childhood friend won for once in Japanese media, LOL

    Reyn had a nice arc of him being Shulk's "big bro" type of character, though he quickly starts to realize it's Shulk who's bailing him out more often than the other way around. It was a nice sequence and moment of maturity from him to decide he wants to be Shulk's equal instead of his protector. Similarly, Melia struggling with her duties as a princess and growing into it by the end was nice to see. Even Riki had a surprising amount of depth when he talks to Dunban about being a father and how hard it is to let go of his kids and let them roam free. Just sucks that a lot of them didn't get too much focus towards the end.

    Zanza was okay as the overarching villain, I do feel like we didn't get enough time from him to properly rate him as a character. Egil is the main villain of most of the game, and in my opinion they're up there for one of the best JRPG villains ever. Not to spoil too much, but perspective is a funny thing, isn't it?

    The world was wonderful, I'm a firm believer in open world zones and this game executed perfectly. Some of the setpieces in the game are stunning, I loved Eryth Sea and night time Satorl Marsh as well as Valak Mountain. It even rewards you for exploring properly, loved that. There's a mass amount of content with side quests, though a lot of them were admitedly unmemorable fetch quests, sadly. I unfortunately think the overwhelming large amount of side content works against the game - it is a little hard to get into, you sort of need to do it to get access to some of the best gear, and not many sidequests are very interesting.

    Combat is one of the most unique systems I've seen in a JRPG, real time combat but also let you cooked up tactics and sequences with chain attacks, it was a great system. The affinity system that created a web of links between your main characters and it affecting chemistry in fights was awesome too. It did also help flesh out some of the characters' personalities, shame it was optional though...

    XC1 might have one of my most favourite OSTs ever, it might be an easier question to ask what theme isn't good, lol. My personal favourites include You Will Know Our Names, Mechanical Rhythm, In the Refugee Camp, and Agniratha. Especially the last one, it's one of the most beautiful themes I've ever heard - the extra strings and piano when they kick in are very powerful. The credits theme as well - Beyond the Sky was beautiful too.

    Overall it was an ambitious game that ran well on a limited piece of hardware, and they delivered a near perfect game. There are things I can't overlook with the game but it really is fantastic. Highly recommend.

The Elder Scrolls III: Morrowind (2002)

PC

Bethesda Game Studios

    I think this is Bethesda's magnum opus.

    Stepping off the boat in Seyda Neen is one of the most magical moments in gaming for me. The plot is legendary, best one Bethesda's ever cooked up in my opinion. The story of you realizing that the "gods" who stabbed you in the back in your previous life are now begging you for help was a sad bit of irony. Especially since the main villain of the game was the one guy who stayed loyal to you for centuries but lost his sanity along the way.

    Dagoth Ur is one of my favourite villains ever. They really make you question whether you're really doing the right thing, just from doing whatever people are telling you to do. I also find it kinda funny that his goal was to make Morrowind great again, quite literally. And while they were kinda doing diabolical things, it's not like their other goals were even that unreasonable. Their monologue right before the boss fight is iconic, and for good reason. One of the greatest villains in games, period.

    I'm sure a lot of people who have heard about this game will know this, if nothing else. The gameplay is absolutely hair rippingly frustrating at first. PC

4A Games

Hitman: World of Assassination (2016/2018/2021)

PC

    Hitman WoA is a very interesting set of games. I feel like a lot of people have misconceptions about this game, it's not exactly a stealth sim shooter like Splinter Cell or tells a thought-provoking story like MGS. This trilogy is very much a sandbox puzzle game with stealth elements and a loosely connected plot. This game does not require crazy mechanics or incredibly fast hands, it rewards patience, timing, creativity and map knowledge. In other words, a speedrunners paradise. And these games are hard. Like, I'm 99th+ percentile in the majority of maps or a bit better, and I feel like the difference between me and the people at the top is the same as the difference in skill between me and someone starting out, those guys are just that fucking good at the game and so creative, I'm jealous.

    It's definitely not a game for everyone. But it's a series that I fell in love with, and I hope someone out there picks up these games and falls in love with it too. If you want to get a taste of what I was able to accomplish, you can check out some of my runs here.

    Paris:
    Fantastic map, I loved it. It's straightforward with the mansion, has tiers/levels of rising security intuitively, and ways to completely circumvent disguises if you know the level well. It rewards you the more time you put in it. Margolis and Novikov were both really fun targets to take out, lots of options no matter what, even on SASO. So many ways to stop the show, after all.
    9.5/10

    Sapienza:
    Top 3 map in the trilogy; the Amalfi Coast is beautiful in this game. The map is the biggest strength, even though large parts of it are unused for targets, it's still super fun exploring the town and seeing what easter eggs IO hid. Caruso and De Santis are great targets with good options at any moment on the clock in SASO, the virus sample, not so much. Not enough for me to take off any marks, but it does warp your run a little bit unless you get tricky with explosives.
    10/10

    Marrakech:
    I do love the map and exploring it, but it's big and not like Sapienza. Traversing this map feels so awful, I think it has to do with the ridiculous amounts of crowds and chokepoints. Zaydan is pretty fun to take out in the school - there's quite a few opportunities for him. Strandberg on the other hand, just lol. The Swedish Consulate really is a fortress, huh? SASO is kind of a nightmare on this map if you want to do it fast.
    7/10

    Bangkok:
    I hate this map, it feels like a House of Cards in Blood Money, but worse. The map genuinely is big, but there's so few options to scale the hotel, so it feels like there's a lot of chokepoints on the map. Morgan and Cross were pretty fun targets though, if you're good with a sniper, this map can be really interesting. That being said, Cross has very limited windows for kills, so a lot of SASO routes feel similar to me.

The race going on made it so that if you wanted to and positioned yourself to, you could kill Sierra at any time. Horizon Zero Dawn (2017)

PS4/PC

    HZD felt like it came out of nowhere, and it was a shockingly great game. Robot Dinosaurs!

    Plot was fantastic. It was immediately obvious that the world you were in was some sort of post apocalypse, and the game covers most of Utah and Colorado. The premise of the game is slowly unravelling just what happened for society to evolve backwards, for the most part and it really was compelling. I think overall Aloy is a great character that grows from someone selfish to caring about the world around her, though she is very unlikable throughout some parts of the game, mostly early-mid way through the game. Sylens was a great foil to her, loved how they hated each other because their egos were so similar haha.

    HADES was an interesting villain. I guess he wasn't even really that maliciously evil, he was just an AI who's primary function was to cleanse life and let GAIA start over with creating new life. I do think the bigger villain in the game was Ted Faro, since he basically created the apocalypse all those years ago from his greed lol.

    I think the world was fascinating to explore, and it is one of the prettiest maps I've ever played in a game. That being said, I do wish the locations had a bit more depth to them other than just collect data/memory, but it was still a great open world. Side quests were okay, but were unmemorable for the most part. Combat is where this game shines, the archery and movement in this game is one of the best I've ever played in a game, the movement especially spoiled me when I went back to other games, it's just so ridiculously fluid.

    A game that tried something new on their take of post apocalypse, and it was a very good attempt. They nailed most of the important parts of a great game, but had slightly weaker elements of RPGs. Great game overall.

Resident Evil 2: Remake (2019)

PC

    A lot of people call RE2make of the best remakes ever, and for good reason.

Persona 5/P5 Royal (2016/2021)

PC/PS4

    Persona 5 is a legendary game, most have it as one of the best JRPGs ever. I agree... with some extreme caveats.

    Plot was okay, kinda mixed. The main theme of the game with corruption, vices and standing up to injustice was good at first. But they really ham it in to you that "we live in a society." It's really such a shame, the first palace with Kamoshida is one of the best story arcs I've ever played in a video game and then... it just slowly falls off. The story picks up again with the casino and the deceit there, but the cruise was disappointing and I don't think the story recovers until the final arc.

    Characters... oh man. Hot take, I did not like the Phantom Thieves overall. The trio of Joker, Ann and Ryuji was hilarious and incredible, I also thought Yusuke was funny too with the way he interacted with that trio but everyone after felt like they never interacted with each other. Haru/Makoto/Futaba/Goro did not feel like a cohesive group, it felt very individualistic once they began to join - it was clearly a group that was there only for Joker and the overall goal of the group, and it showed at the end when they split ways without keeping in touch. Which is realistic and I don't think it's a bad thing, but it really does feel weird that most of these guys were barely friends, just coworkers for the most part. As characters, most of them were done well, barring Haru for reasons outside of her control.

    Don't talk to me about the stupid cat, I only tolerated him because his voice actress is Pikachu/Chopper lol

    Gameplay is where this game shines. By far the best JRPG gameplay I've personally played, running around in Tokyo was incredible. The dungeon crawling was fantastic with the puzzles, they even added some platforming too. Recruiting personas using dialogue was cool though it got kinda annoying after a bit. Lots of quality of life changes to the Velvet Room, all of which was great. Combat is... well, easy. They introduced side arms, showtimes and baton passes which makes for more interesting combos and sequences, but... they just make the game so fucking easy. Adding Nuke and Psy elements was fine, I could've lived without it but it was nice having more weaknesses to exploit.

    Shido was a terrible disappointment as the "main villain," it just felt like he folded so easily, which I guess makes sense with the events that happen immediately after, but you'd think they'd have put more effort into the guy who ruined Joker's life. Yaldy on the other hand... was incredible. I never saw his twist of being fake Igor pulling the strings behind all the major palaces coming, he was a fantastic final boss.

    The DLC that revolved around Maruki, Kasumi and Goro after the ending of P5 was good overall. I liked all three of them, and thought their arcs were done well. I do like to think that the song "Our Light" is about Goro, his relationship with Joker throughout the game and how he represented Joker if he chose to become a villain instead of an anti-hero was always very fascinating to me.

    This game did a lot right. But they failed to nail the one thing that makes Persona games unique. A strong main cast whose stories you get invested in. And for that reason, I can't put this game up there as high as some people. I do think it is the best overall game of the series, just not... the best "Persona" game, if that makes any sense.
